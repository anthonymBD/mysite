When Their Website Got Hacked, I Built Them Something Better in 48 Hours

The Challenge: Matilda Jane's website was hacked and completely offline. Customers couldn't browse dresses. Revenue was at a standstill.

The Solution: I built a streamlined database that made finding dresses easier than the original site ever was.

The Result: A working prototype delivered in 48 hours that customers actually prefer over the old website.

See it live: Matilda Jane Database

The Problem: Complex Website, Frustrated Customers

The original Matilda Jane website had a serious usability problem even before it got hacked. Customers reported that finding specific dresses was like searching through a cluttered closet. Too many steps, too much scrolling, and filters that didn't work intuitively.

When security issues took the entire site offline, they needed a solution fast. But instead of just rebuilding what existed, I saw an opportunity to fix what was broken.

My Approach: Strip Away Everything That Doesn't Help

I started by asking one simple question: What do customers actually need to find and buy a dress?

The answer led to three core features:

  • Visual browsing - See dresses immediately, not buried under navigation menus
  • Smart filtering - Find what you want by size, color, or style in one click
  • Clean layout - No distractions, just dresses and essential information

Every design decision came back to that question. If a feature didn't help customers find and buy dresses faster, it didn't make the cut.

Why This "Quick Fix" Outperforms the Original Site

Here's what users told me they preferred about the new database:

  • Instant search - No waiting for pages to load or filters to apply
  • Mobile-friendly - Works perfectly on phones where most shopping happens
  • Easy navigation - You can find any dress in under 30 seconds
  • No clutter - The dresses are the hero, everything else is secondary

The prototype was built under emergency conditions, which meant focusing ruthlessly on what mattered most. That constraint actually led to better results than the original feature-bloated site.

What I Learned: Simple Wins

This project reinforced something I see repeatedly: more features don't mean a better user experience. Sometimes the best solution is stripping away complexity and building exactly what users need, nothing more.

When you're under pressure to deliver quickly, you can't afford to waste time on features that don't move the needle. That constraint forces clarity, and clarity creates better products.
